TARget

Ball throwing exertion game for developing aiming skills

TARget is a ball throwing exertion game made by me, for the research group healthy lifestyle in a stimulating environment, with the goal of helping children develop aiming and throwing skills. This project was made using Processing, which is a Java library.

  • Made with Java
  • Fresh design
  • Performed research

The brief

TARget was made during my first internship at the Research Group Healthy Lifestyle in a Stimulating Environment over a 20 week period of time including user testing and research.

The goal of the project was to apply interactive technologies to physical education in order to make physical education classes more efficient, more motivating and more targeted.

The project

Before development of TARget itself commenced research was performed on the current Dutch physical education curriculum and how this was currently being applied in physical education classes. From this a decision was made to focus on the aiming and throwing part of the curriculum. Users are to throw at a variable size target from a distance based on the age of the user and their skill level.

In order to be able to digitally register the throws made by the user the Kinect was used, due to its handy infrared sensor and easy integration with various programming languages and IDEs.

Based on these criteria TARget was created using Processing, an extension for Java, where the user can throw at a target in a variety of gamemodes in order to cater to the development of different skills. The program is divided into multiple classes to facilitate easier interaction between the main logic loop, the individual gamemodes and the Kinect sensor.

The result

At the end of my internship 4 gamemodes were successfully implemented into TARget. A standard mode, where the player can throw against a static target without any feedback. A moving mode where the target changed position on the screen. A size changing mode where the target shrinks or enlarges based on if it is hit or not. Lastly a complementary feedback gamemode which shows the user the exact point where the ball hit the wall using a purple dot.

As of this writing a 5th gamemode was included by way of a conditional shrinking or enlarging gamemode, where players had to hit a target multiple times for it to shrink, and miss multiple times for it to grow.

Secret Motion

Unity VR Leap Motion project

Secret Motion is a VR project developed for the Oculus Rift where the player can interact with the virtual environment using their own hands using a Leap Motion controller mounted on the front of the Oculus Rift. This project was realised using Unity.

  • Made with Unity
  • Made for VR
  • Motion Control

The brief

Secret Motion was created as part of a 2 week project were something had to be created which was related to AR, VR or both in a group situation. We decided we wanted to have users be able to use their own hands whilst in a VR environment. For this we used Unity.

The project

Secret Motion initially started off as a very ambitious project were we wanted to have the player in a fully modelled interactive environment play as a mage and be able to access certain spells by making gestures with their hands. However it quite quickly became apparent that this was not possible in our allocated time.

We pivoted over to creating a simpler interactive environment where the player can interact with various objects and have certain actions happen based on hand and finger position. In order to accomplish this we split up the various elements of the project into its constituent pieces and began animating and programming these. These individual pieces were each tested using one of the Leap Motion sensors. After which we also tested them with the Oculus Rift which was modified to have a Leap Motion sensor mounted in front of it.

After this I took it onto me to gather and combine all of these and make them play nice with each other.

The result

To conclude these 2 weeks an exposition was organised where visitors could visit and interact with the project. The user was positioned behind a line of tape on the ground and had the Oculus Rift placed over the head.

In the virtual environment the user had access to different buttons, that each created their own gameobject. These gameobjects could be moved by using your own hands. The user could throw at a target, topple a wall or a pyramid made of cubes or make fire and lasers appear out of one's hands.

Digital assistant Hendrik

Digital guided assistant and companion

Digital assistant Hendrik is a digital guide and companion for visitors of the Gemeentemuseum Den Haag. This application for mobile phones assists the user during their visit with handy tips and information.

  • Mobile application
  • Fresh design
  • Performed research

The brief

The Gemeentemuseum Den Haag had a problem where they found that people would get lost in the museum and asked us how we could solve this. For this project 14 weeks were allocated, of which 7 week research and 7 design and development.

The project

The project started off with an initial visit to the museum for a look into how we experienced the current museum as it is. Based on this we gathered a list of what we deemed as inadequacies, such as bad signage and not knowing if you visited the entire museum.

Based on this we started interviewing visitors and observing behaviour within the museum. This verified our earlier findings, but also added a requirement for more information about the art. We decided that we would focus on the holistic experience of the museum visit, and gathered more data on that. Such as visitor’s interaction with various touchpoints within the museum and common patterns exhibited by visitors.

Another point we found out was that most people did not know the interesting story of the museum, which was created by Hendrik Berlage in order with the intend of making people feel lost in time whilst in the museum.

After this research we focused on improving the story of Berlage and the museum, increasing the amount of information available to the museum and making the visitor feel fulfilled at the end of the visit.

The result

The final result, named Hendrik, after the creator of the museum building is a digital assistant visitors can download on their phone using the free Wi-Fi at the museum. Hendrik is originally introduced to the visitors via the tickets they order online. These personalised tickets contain a personal message from the museum which guides them to the digital assistant.

Once downloaded and visiting the museum the digital assistant will guide the visitor around using simple and clean digital maps. When the visitor nears a point of interest, upon which the user's phone will draw the attention of the user. The user will be prompted to move the camera of the phone to the point of interest. Visitors subsequently receive information about this point of interest.

Lastly the digital assistant will guide the visitor to the Berlage room where more information about Berlage and his museum is presented using an interactive 3d model.

As the visitors are done with the visit they can keep their personalised tickets as memorabilia.

TomTom Curfer

Redesigned driving statistics app

TomTom Curfer is a project in which I redesigned the currently existing TomTom Curfer, a car driving statistics app, in order to add to the experience of its user. For this a communal element was added into the Curfer to make people compete and compare their scores.

  • Mobile application
  • Redesign
  • Performed research

The brief

TomTom Curfer came out of a request by TomTom to create a redesign for their newest product, the TomTom Curfer, since it didn’t gain much traction in its current state. We were to identify the core issues and create redesigns based on these issues. These redesigns did not have to be fully functioning, but had to show the solution as an interactive prototype. 10 weeks were established for this project including research and design.

The project

The TomTom Curfer is an application on the user’s mobile phone which tracks the data outputted by the car and its internal sensors. Based on this data, the driver is awarded a current score.

The project started with the installation of the TomTom Curfer in my own car. Due to this I could see how it would work. After which I asked 2 users to drive a route for 30 minutes each. This was followed up with an interview with these 2 users. This in order to ask for their experience with the TomTom Curfer and if they would like to use it in the future.

From this I established that the drivers felt no need to use the TomTom Curfer, since they did not the the benefits of using it. The drivers wanted reasons to use the application such as competition and rewards.

After this I met with a car mechanic. He was not familiar with the TomTom Curfer, but was familiar with the technologies contained within. The mechanic did state, that the TomTom Curfer did not offer any new opportunities. Sadly enough no new insights were gathered from this conversation.

The result

Based on the research data I stared redesigning the TomTom Curfer. For this I used Photoshop and Axure RP.

The final redesign of the TomTom Curfer consists of a home screen where your driving score is determined on you average driving score divided by your total distance travelled. Another feature is the group section where people can partake in groups en see a score overview.

The idea behind this is that drivers will better their driving capabilities due to these communal features such as the competition and the possibilities of rewards if they show the best driving skills.
